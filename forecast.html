Landing / Thesis

My main argument

Artificial intelligence is often described as if it will suddenly remove everyone’s job. I think that is too simple. For Australia, the more realistic change by 2030 is that AI will reorganise tasks inside many jobs. Some workers will become faster and more productive, especially in services, finance, education, health administration, government and professional work. At the same time, the gains may not be shared equally.

This website is inspired by AI 2027. That project presents a fast-moving scenario where advanced AI systems appear earlier than many people expect, including expert-human-level AI systems in early 2027 and a pathway toward much more capable systems by the end of 2027. I do not treat this as a guaranteed prediction. I use it as a stress test: if AI capability moves faster than normal policy systems, Australia needs to prepare before the labour market pressure becomes obvious.

My thesis is that AI could lift Australia’s productivity by 2030, but only if adoption is matched with skills, trust and regulation. If Australia only focuses on cost-cutting, AI could increase inequality, reduce entry-level opportunities and create more workplace surveillance.

Plain version: AI is not just a job destroyer. It is more like a task reshaper. The real question is who gets the benefit: workers, firms, consumers, or only the largest technology adopters.
Capabilities → Economic Channels

How AI capability becomes an economic impact

AI does not affect the economy by magic. It works through channels. In this forecast, I focus on four channels: productivity, task automation, wages and industry structure.

1. Productivity

AI can help workers write, code, research, summarise, analyse data and answer customers faster. This can increase output per hour, which is important because weak productivity growth has been a long-running concern in Australia.

2. Task automation

AI changes tasks before it changes full jobs. A legal assistant, accountant or marketing worker may keep the same job title, but part of their daily work may be done with AI support.

3. Wages and inequality

Workers who can use AI well may become more valuable. Workers without training may face weaker bargaining power, more monitoring or fewer junior learning opportunities.

4. Industry structure

Large firms can adopt AI faster because they have data, money and technical teams. Small businesses may benefit too, but only if tools are affordable and rules are clear.

Evidence I use

The Australian Bureau of Statistics reported that business AI R&D spending rose to $668.3 million in 2023–24, compared with $276.3 million in 2021–22. This suggests Australian firms are already increasing investment in AI, even before many labour market effects are fully visible. The Australian Government’s National AI Plan also frames AI as part of a more competitive, productive and resilient economy.

Scenarios 2027–2030

Three possible futures

The point of scenarios is not to guess one perfect future. It is to show what changes when the assumptions change. I use three scenarios: baseline, upside and downside.

Baseline: uneven adoption

AI adoption grows steadily but unevenly. Banks, large firms, universities and government agencies use AI more quickly. Small businesses and less resourced workers adopt AI more slowly.

Likely result: moderate productivity gains, but a wider gap between AI-confident workers and workers who receive little training.

Upside: productivity with inclusion

Australia invests early in practical AI training, safe data access, public-sector capability and small business support. AI reduces paperwork and helps workers focus on higher-value tasks.

Likely result: stronger productivity growth, better service delivery and lower inequality risk.

Downside: fast adoption without protection

AI capability improves quickly, closer to the warning-style future in AI 2027. Firms use AI mainly to cut costs. Junior roles shrink and workplace monitoring increases.

Likely result: short-run efficiency, but weaker trust, higher insecurity and fewer entry-level learning pathways.

Policy Playbook

What Australia should do

Australia should not respond to AI by banning it, but it should also not allow a “use it first, fix problems later” approach. My preferred approach is controlled adoption: encourage useful AI while protecting workers, privacy and fairness.

Policy actions

Action Why it matters Possible KPI
Fund practical AI training for students, workers and small business owners AI benefits are larger when workers know how to use tools safely and effectively Share of workers completing recognised AI training
Require transparency in high-risk AI decisions Workers and citizens should know when AI is used in hiring, performance reviews or public services Number of agencies/firms publishing AI transparency statements
Support small business adoption Otherwise productivity gains may be captured mainly by large firms Small business AI adoption rate and reported time saved
Protect entry-level learning pathways If AI removes junior tasks, young workers may lose the chance to learn basic professional skills Graduate and entry-level hiring rates in exposed industries
Keep human review for high-impact decisions AI errors and bias can create real economic and social harm Share of high-risk decisions with documented human review
Methods, Data and Limitations

How I built this forecast

This forecast uses scenario analysis instead of one fixed prediction. I combine a fast AI capability scenario from AI 2027 with Australian evidence from government and policy sources. I also create simple indices to show the mechanisms visually.

Data and assumptions

Input How I use it Limitation
AI 2027 timeline and scenario Used as a stress test for fast AI capability growth It is a forecast scenario, not a guaranteed future
ABS business AI R&D spending Shows that Australian firms are already increasing AI-related investment R&D spending does not directly measure job loss or productivity
Productivity Commission AI opportunity paper Supports the idea that trust, data access and capability shape AI productivity gains Policy effects are hard to measure before adoption is mature
OECD work on AI and workplaces Used to discuss privacy, worker consultation and workplace pressure OECD evidence is often cross-country, not Australia-only

Uncertainty

The biggest uncertainty is the speed of AI capability growth. If AI progresses slowly, the baseline scenario is more likely. If AI progresses quickly, Australia could face pressure similar to the downside scenario unless policy moves earlier. Another uncertainty is that job exposure is not the same as job loss. A job can be exposed to AI and still grow if AI makes the service cheaper, faster or higher quality.

I also recognise that my charts use simplified scores. They are not official government forecasts. Their purpose is to make the causal logic clear: capability affects tasks, tasks affect productivity and wages, and policy changes how the gains and risks are distributed.

AI-enabled methods disclosure

I used ChatGPT to brainstorm the structure, simplify the language and generate early ideas for charts, scenarios and policy pathways. I reviewed, edited and reorganised the content myself. I checked the main evidence against external sources, including AI 2027, the Australian Bureau of Statistics, the Productivity Commission, the OECD and Australian Government materials. The final thesis, assumptions, scenario choices and website design decisions are my own.
